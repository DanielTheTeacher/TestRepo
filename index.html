Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly changing the world – including how we work and learn. For those of you in vocational studies, especially our Elektro-students, it's important to understand what AI is, how you can use it smartly, and the new opportunities and challenges it brings.

This page is designed to help you with just that! Here you will learn about:

The goal is not just for you to be able to use AI tools, but for you to become a criticalOne who thinks for themselves and asks questions., ethicalOne who considers what is right and wrong., and adaptableOne who easily learns new things. user of AI. Let's explore together!

1. Explore AI: What is it, and where do we find it?

What is Artificial Intelligence (AI)?

Think of AI as computers or programs that can do things that usually require human intelligence. This can be learning from experience, solving problems, making decisions, or understanding language.

An important part of AI is Machine Learning (ML)Systems that learn from large amounts of data to find patterns and make decisions without being specifically programmed for each task.. For example, streaming services use Machine Learning (ML) to suggest movies.[1] Watch: What is ML?

Large Language Models (LLMs)Advanced "computer brains" trained on enormous amounts of text and code. are a type of AI you might have heard of, like ChatGPT. They learn patterns and can generate text, answer questions.[2] Important: LLMs don't "think" like humans; they predict the next word based on learned patterns.[2] Watch: What are LLMs?

AI is not magic!

It's important to remember that AI works by recognizing and copying patterns from training data; they have no real understanding or consciousness.[3] This is key to understanding both what AI can do, and what it *cannot* do. Watch: AI - Pattern Recognition vs. Understanding

AI in your daily life and future profession

AI is not just something for the future – it's already part of your everyday life! You encounter AI when using social media (personalized feed)[4], email (spam filter)[5], GPS apps (route optimization)[6], and smart speakers.

AI in Vocational Fields (Focus: Electrical Trades & Computer Technology):

AI is becoming a bigger deal in many jobs, especially in technical fields like Electrical Trades and Computer Technology. It's showing up everywhere, helping with tasks like smart power management, automatically finding faults in circuits, improving cybersecurity, and even assisting with coding. More: AI in Electrical Engineering More: AI in Predictive Maintenance

In most professions, AI is about enhancingMaking better or stronger. human skills, increasing efficiency and safety, not necessarily replacing people. It's important to see how AI can become a useful tool in your specific field!

Why is AI literacy important for you?

AI literacyThe ability to understand basic AI concepts, critically evaluate AI systems, and use AI tools effectively and ethically. is more than just a technical skill. It's a key competency for the future. Read: Why AI Literacy is Important

  • Future-proof your career: AI is changing the job market. With AI literacy, you are better equipped for changes.
  • Make informed choices: Understand how AI affects society, including ethics, privacy, and possible biasUnfair leaning towards or against something or someone, often based on pre-existing ideas instead of facts..
  • Become a better professional: AI literacy helps you think critically and learn throughout life, which is important in all professions.

Being able to assess the information you get from AI tools is especially important. In many professions, important decisions are made. If an AI tool provides incorrect or biased information, you must be able to ask questions and check the information, not just accept it.

2. Think Critically: Navigating an AI-Driven World

How AI Learns (and Gets it Wrong): Bias In, Bias Out

AI models learn from data; if data contains bias, AI will reflect/amplify it.[7] Watch: AI Bias Explained Watch: How Training Data Creates Bias

Examples of AI bias that could affect you:

  • Dangerous "Facts" for Your Trade: Imagine you're an Elektro-student looking up safety rules for high-voltage installations. An AI might give you an answer in perfect Norwegian, claiming these are "Norwegian electrical safety standards." But, if the AI was mostly trained on American data, it might actually be giving you US electrical codes, just translated. Following these could be extremely dangerous in Norway, as safety standards differ, potentially leading to accidents, equipment damage, or even electrocution. The AI sounds correct, but it's dangerously wrong for your specific location and rules.
  • Getting Skipped for an Opportunity (And No One Knows Why): Let's say in a few years you apply for a summer job or a special program. The company uses AI to sort through applications. Your application might get skipped. It could be that the AI was poorly designed to promote diversity and ended up accidentally filtering *out* male applicants for certain roles, or it might unfairly filter out female candidates for technical roles if it learned from historical data where mostly men held those positions. Or it might be looking for tiny, weird patterns in CVs that it thinks predict success, but actually have nothing to do with how good you'd be, accidentally discriminating against you.
  • Social Media Misunderstandings & Shadow Bans: You post something online – a joke, an opinion, a comment. An AI moderator, trained mostly on English internet drama or American cultural issues, might misunderstand your Norwegian slang, sarcasm, or cultural reference. It could flag your normal comment as "problematic," delete it, or even temporarily ban your account ("shadow ban" you so fewer people see your stuff) because it wrongly thinks you're breaking rules, even when you're just talking like a typical Norwegian teen.

For your vocational field, a biased AI could lead to incorrect diagnoses, unfair assessments, or designs that don't suit everyone. It's important to remember that AI bias often mirrors existing societal inequalitiesUnfair differences in how groups of people are treated or have access to opportunities in society..[7] Therefore, human oversight and critical assessment are always needed.

AI "Hallucinations" & Looks Real, But Isn't: Spotting Convincing Fakes

Sometimes AI can generate incorrect or misleading information presented as fact (AI hallucinations).[2] This is called AI hallucinationsWhen AI invents facts or information that isn't true.. Read: AI Hallucinations Explained

Why does this happen? AI hallucinations occur because AI doesn't 'know' but predicts based on patterns.[2] Think of it like a super-advanced autocomplete. If you ask it, 'Who will win the Ballon d'Or in 2025?', it doesn't know the answer. But it knows which football players' names often appear near 'Ballon d'Or winner'. So, it might confidently name a famous current player. It sounds believable because the name is right, the context is right, but the AI is just making a highly probable guess, not stating a known fact. It can be totally wrong.

This isn't deliberate deception by the AI. It's just how it works: predicting patterns. If the patterns in its data are incomplete or lead to a 'likely' but false statement, it will still generate it.

For example, an AI might 'invent' historical events or scientific 'facts.' The problem is made worse because AI can make its output look very real and professional (what I, Daniel the teacher, like to call 'the look of expertise'). Perfect grammar and a confident tone can make false information seem true, even when it's completely made up.

Misinformation, Echo Chambers & Filter Bubbles

On the internet, false or inaccurate information can spread quickly. It's important to distinguish between:

  • MisinformationFalse info spread, often without the sender knowing it's false.
  • DisinformationFalse info created and spread intentionally to deceive or harm.
Learn: Mis/Disinformation & Fact-Checking

AI can amplify these problems. Algorithms on social media can create:

  • Echo ChambersEnvironments where you mostly see opinions you already agree with, and alternative views are rarely encountered.
  • Filter BubblesWhen algorithms personalize the content you see based on your past behavior, so you miss other perspectives, often without knowing it.
Read: Filter Bubbles (Wikipedia) Watch: Echo Chambers & Filter Bubbles

This is related to the Attention EconomyThe idea that your attention is a valuable resource that websites and apps compete for, often through advertising.. AI is good at finding and promoting content that captures attention, regardless of whether it's true or good for you.

The Dangers are Real: This isn't just about being wrong; it's serious. Believing and spreading false AI-generated information can have severe consequences. In your future jobs, decisions based on bad info could lead to dangerous mistakes, financial loss, or harm to people's reputations or even their safety. Imagine an electrician relying on faulty AI-generated wiring diagrams, or a healthcare assistant acting on incorrect AI medical advice. Online, it can lead to bullying, scams, and even influence important societal events like elections with false narratives, undermining democratic processes. Being critical isn't just a skill; it's a responsibility to protect yourself and others.

3. AI as Helper: Use the Tools Smartly and Responsibly

The "You First" Principle: Your Brain, Then AI

When you use AI for school, the most important rule is: You First! This means you must always start by using your own brain – your own thinking, planning, and initial effort – before you turn to AI.

Think of it this way: using AI to write your essays or solve all your problems is like using a forklift to lift weights at the gym. Sure, the forklift can lift heavy things easily, but you don't get any stronger. The whole point of lifting weights is to build your own muscles. Similarly, the point of doing schoolwork isn't just to get an answer or a finished product; it's to build your "mental muscles."

When you write an essay, for example, you're not just learning to write. You're learning much deeper skills: how to structure your thoughts, organize complex information, think critically about a topic, find evidence, and communicate your ideas clearly and persuasively. These are incredibly important skills for any job and for life in general. If AI does this heavy lifting for you, you miss out on developing these critical abilities.

Learning can sometimes feel a bit tough or "painful" – that's when your brain is really working hard and growing. It's often at these moments that it's most tempting to let AI take over. But remember the saying, "no pain, no gain." That struggle is actually what learning feels like. If you skip that struggle, you skip the learning.

Scientists have noticed that many teenagers today report feeling more stressed and perhaps less able to tackle difficulties. Some think this might be partly because technology, including AI, can remove many of the small challenges and hurdles that actually help us grow stronger and more resilient. Your brain is incredibly plasticAble to change and adapt as a result of experience. (able to change and adapt) when you're young – roughly until your early twenties.[11] This is the prime time to build those fundamental thinking skills. If you rely too much on AI to do the thinking for you now, it can be much harder to develop these essential skills later in life.

So, always start with your own effort. Once you've wrestled with the material yourself, then AI can be a useful tool to supportTo assist or hold up., improveTo make better., or refineTo make small changes to improve something. your work. But you do the core thinking first.

AI for AugmentationTo add to or enhance something, making it better or more effective. vs. Generation

There's a big difference between using AI to augment your work and letting AI generate work for you.

Generation (Risky for Learning)

AI creates content or solutions almost entirely from scratch, often based on a simple command (prompt), without much prior effort from you. This is like letting the forklift do all the lifting – you don't build your own skills.

Example: "Write an essay about..."

Augmentation (Good for Learning)

AI helps you, improves, or builds upon work you've already started or thought out. AI is a collaborator, helping you lift more after you've done your reps.

Example: "Here's a paragraph I wrote. Can you give feedback on clarity and grammar?"

In school and professional life, the focus should be on augmentation. AI can help you see new angles, improve what you've created, or automate simple parts of a task, so you can focus on the more demanding and creative parts.

Ethical Use: AI as a Learning Partner (with Norwegian Guidelines)

With the "You First" principle and your teacher's permission, AI can be a great learning partner. Here are some examples of ethical AI use: Ethical AI Use (NDLA) Norwegian AI Guidelines (Udir)

  • Brainstorming (after your own effort!): This is key: after you have already thought deeply about your project and have your own initial ideas, you could ask AI: "I've thought about X, Y, Z for my project. Can you suggest some other angles or related concepts I might have missed?" Using AI before you've done your own thinking is like asking the forklift to decide which muscles you should train – it defeats the purpose.
  • Getting feedback on YOUR work: "Can you check this text I wrote for grammar mistakes?"
  • Explaining difficult topics (after your own effort): "I've read about Ohm's Law but struggle to understand. Can you explain it simply for an apprentice in electrical trades?"
  • Practicing skills: "Give me a scenario for a customer conversation where the customer is dissatisfied."

Important in Norway:

  • AI as support, not replacement: AI should support learning, not replace your own work and critical thinking.
  • Be transparent: Be clear about when and how you've used AI.
  • Check facts: Information from AI must always be checked against reliable sources. Do not cite AI as a source.
  • Privacy: Don't share sensitive personal information or copyrighted material with AI tools, especially those not approved for GDPR (privacy regulations). Consider using tools like NDLA AI Chat, which is adapted to Norwegian privacy laws.[12]

Ethical use is about your intention and transparency. The goal must be to learn more and become better, not to avoid work.

Academic Integrity: What's NOT Okay? – Consequences of Cheating with AI

Using AI to do your work for you and presenting it as your own is considered cheating. Schools take this very seriously. The following information about what constitutes cheating with AI and its consequences is paraphrased and inspired by guidelines such as those from Vestfold Fylke (see Vestfold Fylke's rules). Similar rules and consequences apply in Møre og Romsdal and other regions.

Hva regnes som juks og plagiat med KI?

Juks med KI innebærer å presentere KI-generert arbeid som ditt eget, uten tillatelse eller uten å ha gjort en betydelig egeninnsats først. Dette inkluderer direkte avskrift eller omskrivinger fra KI uten kildehenvisning. Selv om KI kan være et nyttig verktøy for læring og refleksjon, er det juks hvis du bruker det til å unngå eget arbeid og presenterer resultatet som ditt.

Reaksjoner ved juks og plagiat med KI (paraphrased from typical school guidelines):

Å jukse med KI kan få alvorlige følger, avhengig av hvor alvorlig det er og om det har skjedd før.

  • Du kan få IV (Ikke Vurdert) på prøver, innleveringer, og i verste fall i standpunktkarakter i faget (jf. forskrift til opplæringsloven §§ 9-14, 9-17).
  • Ved alvorlig eller gjentatt juks kan du få nedsatt karakter i orden og/eller oppførsel (jf. «skoleregler for elever, § 5-3»). Skolen vil da fatte et enkeltvedtak, og du (og foresatte hvis du er under 18) blir varslet og får forklare deg.
  • Juks på eksamen kan føre til at eksamen blir annullert, og du mister også standpunktkarakteren i faget. Dette kan bety at du ikke får førstegangsvitnemål (jf. forskrift til opplæringsloven § 9-42).

Hovedpoenget er: KI skal ikke erstatte din egen læring, kritiske tenkning eller kreativitet. Målet med skolen er at du skal lære og utvikle disse ferdighetene.

5. AI & Society: Your Responsibility as a User and Citizen

The Attention Economy and AI's Role

The Attention EconomyThe idea that your attention is valuable, and tech companies design products to capture it, often for advertising revenue. is about your attention being valuable, and tech companies design products to capture it, often for advertising revenue.[13] Read: The Attention Economy (Text) Watch: The Attention Economy (Video)

AI is an engine in this:[14]

  • Personalization: AI algorithms tailor content (feeds, recommendations, notifications) to be as engaging as possible for you.
  • Engagement Optimization: AI can identify and promote content that captures attention (e.g., emotional, sensational), regardless of quality or accuracy.

Once your attention is captured, you become more susceptible to manipulation. Misinformation and disinformation can more easily take root when you're already engaged and perhaps less critical. Your values can also be subtly shaped. For example, you might see a "cringe" video of a woman with brightly colored hair who is vegan and says something outrageous. Even if the video is designed purely to provoke anger and get views (thereby making money for the creator), repeated exposure to such content could unconsciously affect your attitude towards people with colored hair, vegans, or people holding certain political opinions. The goal of such content is often just to make you react, not to inform or engage in good faith.

Disadvantages can include increased distraction, impact on mental health, and spread of misleading content. For you as a vocational student, it's important to protect your focus for learning and future work.

Writing Prompts 4: Ethical Dilemmas in AI

As a professional, you might face ethical dilemmas where AI recommendations clash with your judgment or professional ethics. Consider the following scenarios: More: AI Ethics Case Studies

Scenario 1: Electrical Safety

An AI tool for electrical planning suggests a wiring layout for a complex industrial machine that meets basic code but seems overly complicated and difficult to maintain safely. Your experience suggests a simpler, more robust layout would be better in the long run, though it might use slightly more expensive components initially.

Prompt 4.1: For the Electrical Safety scenario, what would you do? Explain your reasoning. How do you balance the AI's suggestion (which is code-compliant and possibly cost-effective short-term) with your professional judgment about long-term safety and maintainability? What are the potential consequences of each choice?

Scenario 2: System Automation

An AI system designed to optimize energy consumption in a smart building frequently overrides manual settings made by occupants for comfort (e.g., slightly adjusting temperature or lighting). The AI achieves documented energy savings, but occupants complain about a lack of control and discomfort.

Prompt 4.2: For the System Automation scenario, what are the ethical considerations? How do you balance energy efficiency goals with user comfort and autonomy? If you were the technician responsible for this system, what steps might you take to address the conflict?

Such dilemmas often have no easy answers. They show how important human judgment, ethical reflection, and the ability to critically question AI are.

Your Voice Counts: Contribute to Ethical AI Development

You're not just a passive user of AI – you can help shape how AI is developed and used ethically!

  • Give feedback: When using AI tools, provide constructive criticism about performance, bias, or usability.
  • Promote ethical practices: In your future job, advocate for ethical AI principles and responsible use.
  • Be mindful of the data you create: The data you generate online and through your interactions with AI can be used to train future AI models.
  • Join the dialogue: Engage in discussions about AI, such as in student councils or professional forums. Community input is valuable.

  • Discussing AI in Your Trade (Ch 6, "Skills Focus: Discussing Vocational Topics", p. 272): This framework is excellent for class discussions about AI's role in specific trades like Electrical Engineering or Computer Technology.
  • AI as a New Tool (Ch 5, "Tools of the Trade", p. 200): Frame AI as one of the new "Tools of the Trade." How does it compare to physical tools? What new "maintenance" (i.e., critical thinking, fact-checking) does this digital tool require?
  • Safety, Standards & Human Responsibility (Ch 2, "Safety First!", pp. 54-55 & "Codes and Standards", pp. 76-77): When discussing "Ethical Judgment & Responsibility" as a human skill, relate it to the critical importance of safety and standards in electrical trades. AI might suggest something, but human judgment, knowledge of codes (like NEK 400), and responsibility for safety are paramount. An AI won't take responsibility for an accident.
  • AI in Vocational Scenarios (Various "Core Content" texts): Texts like "The Rule of Three" (Ch 2, pp. 64-65 - loss of digital infrastructure), "On Call-Out" (Ch 6, pp. 250-251 - technician problem-solving), or "Spare Parts" (Ch 6, pp. 258-259 - students building ROV) can be springboards. Discussion: How would AI change these scenarios? What human skills shown by the characters would still be vital?

Connections for "5. AI & Society"

  • Advocacy & Shaping AI (Ch 4, "Young Activists", pp. 192-193): When discussing "Your Voice Counts," these examples show how individuals can advocate for change. This can inspire you to be active in shaping ethical AI.
